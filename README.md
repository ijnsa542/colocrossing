## What They Offer

ColoCrossing Cloud keeps the menu short and honest — two product lines, both with genuinely competitive specs.

**Cloud VPS** — KVM-based virtual private servers, SSD storage, Linux and Windows templates available, centralized control panel. Every plan includes 20TB monthly bandwidth, 1Gbps port speed, and a 100% uptime SLA. Good for developers, small businesses, side projects, VPN nodes, and pretty much anything where you want isolated resources without paying DigitalOcean prices.

**Cloud Metal Servers** — This is the more interesting one. Same fast cloud provisioning and control panel as the VPS line, but with *dedicated* CPU cores rather than shared. Think of it as the midpoint between a regular VPS and a full dedicated server. If you've ever dealt with noisy neighbor issues on shared VPS hardware — unpredictable spikes, sluggish disk, that annoying moment when someone else's workload tanks your response times — Cloud Metal is the cure. Great for databases, game servers, anything CPU-sensitive.

## What Real Users Say

The honest picture on ColoCrossing is a mixed bag — which actually makes sense when you understand the business.

**On the dedicated server / colocation side:** Reviews here tend to be glowing. A 15-year customer wrote that uptime had been "remarkable" and they honestly couldn't remember the last unscheduled outage. Multiple long-term customers mention fast, personalized support from account managers who actually know their setup.

**On the cloud VPS side:** More variable. A subset of users at specific locations (Dallas gets mentioned more than once) have reported extended outages and terse support responses. On the flip side, a lot of reviews call out how fast tickets get answered — sometimes within 30 seconds for routine issues.

The pattern that emerges: if you pick a well-established location like Buffalo or Chicago for cloud workloads, experiences tend to be better. Dallas seems to have had more incidents. For non-critical workloads, side projects, dev environments, or test servers, the price-to-performance ratio is genuinely hard to argue with.
